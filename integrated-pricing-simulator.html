OD Rank

1. What is OD Rank?

OD Rank reflects how expensive a corridor typically is, relative to others, after removing the effects of seasonality and departure timing.

  • 0 = Lowest-priced OD (usually main-port ↔ main-port)
  • 1 = Highest-priced OD in the trade

2. Why it matters:

This ranking helps you apply consistent pricing logic across lanes. It's like a "price fingerprint" for each OD which is stable, explainable, and fair.

3. How it's used:

The main pricing chart uses OD Rank on the X-axis to show how actual prices compare to predicted and target prices, helping you spot gaps and set strategy.

Term Structure

1. What is Term Structure?

Term structure shows how contract rates relate to spot rates over different commitment periods. It answers: "What's a fair price to lock in for 1 month vs 3 months?"

2. How we calculate each rate:

  • Spot Rate = The simulated market price for that specific week
  • 1-Month Contract = Average of the next 4 weeks of spot rates
    Formula: (Week N + Week N+1 + Week N+2 + Week N+3) ÷ 4
  • 3-Month Contract = Average of all remaining weeks (up to 13)
    Formula: (Week N + Week N+1 + ... + Week 49) ÷ remaining weeks

3. Why it matters:

These represent "fair breakeven" rates — the contract price where, on average, you'd be indifferent between locking in a contract vs staying on spot.

  • If the market is rising (bullish), contract rates will be higher than current spot
  • If the market is falling (bearish), contract rates will be lower than current spot
  • 3-Month is typically more stable than 1-Month (averages over more weeks)

4. Example:

If Week 37 spot is $3,000 and the market is expected to rise to $3,200 by Week 49:

  • 1-Month contract ≈ $3,030 (average of weeks 37-40)
  • 3-Month contract ≈ $3,100 (average of weeks 37-49)

A customer locking in at these rates would, on average, pay the same as if they'd stayed on spot — no advantage either way.
